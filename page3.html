Chess Pieces and How They Move

Now that you have learned how to set up the board, you need to know how each piece actually works and moves.

Each piece has a different way of moving, and some even have their own unique mechanics, which will be explained in the next page.

Pieces have their worth calculated in points, to better help you understand whether you should trade a piece (sacrificing a piece for another) or to help you understand if you are winning or losing.

The Pawn

There are 16 pawns in total. 8 white and 8 black, for each player. Pawns are considered the least powerful piece by itself, and also the least valuable, with a value of 1 point. This is because it can only be moved 1 square up at a time, with the exception of a 2 square move on its first move from its starting position. Howevever, pawns can only use this move twice, and it is not required for it to move 2 spaces up initially, as players can choose to move it up 1 instead. Pawns also can move diagonally up right/left when capturing another piece. Pawns can only capture in this way, and if it meets another piece, then it is essentially stuck, as it cannot move sideways and only up, until another piece comes on its upper diagonal positions.

The Bishop

There are 2 bishops per player, each having a bishop located on the white and black squares. The bishop can only move diagonally, up or down, left or right. However it cannot move horizontally or vertically, meaning that a bishop located on the white squares will forever be stuck on white squares only. However this is why you have 2 bishops, one to control the black squares, and one to control the white squares. The bishops are worth 3 points each.

The Knight

Each player has 2 knights. Knights move in an L-shape, two squares in one direction and then one square perpendicular to that direction. Knights are worth 3 points each.

The Rook

Each player has 2 rooks. Rooks can move horizontally or vertically any number of squares, with the ability to move back and forth as well. However they cannot move diagonally and are limited by other pieces. Rooks are best for endgame, as in the beginning of the game, there are pawns and too many pieces in general blocking the Rook's pathways. The Rook is worth 5 points.

The Queen

The queen is the most powerful piece on the board, with the ability to move in any direction, combining the movements of the rook and bishop. However each player only gets 1 queen each, and they cannot be moved in a way like the knights do. The queen is worth 9 points.

The King

Each player has 1 king. It is considered the most powerful piece in the board. You technically cannot capture/kill the king, but essentially you can check it if the king is in danger from any other piece. (This is done by placing the king in a position where it could be captured by another piece on their next move.) The king is worth infinatly many points, and should be protected at all costs.

If the king is checked, then the king is forced to move, or if that is not possible, then another piece must be moved in order to protect the king. If neither of those are possible, then you get checkmated and lose.
