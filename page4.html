Chess Mechanics

The Pawn

The pawn as explained before, can only move up one square at a time, with the exception of being able to capture diagonally up only, and the first move having the option of being 2 squares up.

However, if the pawn somehow moves to the other side of the board, or the top, the pawn will then ahve the option of becoming another piece. It could change to anything except for the king. It could change to a knight, a bishop, a rook, or even a queen. This is what makes the pawn especially threatening while it is pushing up the board.

Unfortunatly if you are playing chess physically, some sets do not have enough pieces for you to be able to promote your pawn, which is why you may have to get creative and search for alternatives. However this problem does not exist on online chess, which is one of the main advantages of online chess.

En Passant

En Passant is French for "in passing". If a pawn moves out two squares on its first move and by doing so it lands next to an opponent's pawn, the opponent's pawn has the option of capturing the first pawn as it passes by. (Picture demonstrated below)

Castling

Castling is a special move that can be done if there are special requirements met:

  1. The king has not moved yet.
  2. The rook has not been moved yet.
  3. There cannot be any pieces in between the king and the rook.
  4. The king may not be in check or pass through check.
How does it work?

If all of the following conditions have been met, the Rook can essentially move to protect the King.

The king moves two squares toward the rook, in the direction of the rook you chose to castle with. The Rook then moves one square to the other side of the king.

Why is this useful?

This can be used in case the king is in danger from another piece, and castling is the only way out, or to get the Rook out to put more pressure.

Tips/Tricks

Chess is a game full of strategy and tactics. Even if you know all the rules, that doesn't guarentee a victory. You need to stratigically think ahead and calculate potential moves or mistakes that your opponents might make.

Basic Strategies

There are some simple things that every chess player should know. These tips will drastically help you improve your game sense:

Protect Your King

The king is literally your only way of losing. You must protect him at all costs. Do not play risky and put him out in the open. Putting him in the corner of the board where he is less vulnerable is a good strategy. If you do get checked, opponents might use that opportunity where you are forced to move your king in order to capture another valuable piece like your queen.

Know your pieces and their strengths

Pieces are each calculated in terms of their value, which is based off of their in game strenghts and weaknesses. The official syste, calculates a pawn worth as 1, a knight and bishop as 3, a rook as 5, and the queen as 9. This can let you take a grasp on what to take and what not to take. For example, trading a Rook for a Knight isn't the smartest. Even though both opponents have 2 of each, the Rook is more valuable as it has more mobility and poses more of a threat, and can be used offensivly when both rooks are present in order to try to force a "Ladder Checkmate" (Checkmating strategy where both rooks push the king)

Control the Center

Getting your pieces out to control the board gives you a greater advantage, as you will have more romo to move your pieces, making it harder for your opponents to find a safe square and take control, making it easier for you to capture pieces without any risks or losses.

Try to develop your pieces; get them out and in a threatening position. They will not help you at all if they are just sitting in the back doing nothing. A bishop has control of a lot of board, and can catch an unsuspecting piece from far away off guard if there is nothing blocking its way.

Don't be afraid to sacrifice pieces

Sometimes, taking an even or losing trade isn't the end of the world. Expensive pieces can be used as bait to take advantage of a potential vurnarability from your opponent. For example, you can put out a bishop to try to get rid of a queen's defensive pawn, allowing your knight to easily capture the queen for an even better trade.

Practice, Practice, Practice!

Playing lots of games will help you develop your gamesense and skills. Reflect on your losses or victories to improve your strategy and figure out what went wrong for you or your opponent. Reflect and analyzing will help you develop your gamesense of what to do and what not to do.
